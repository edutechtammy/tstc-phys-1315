  Scientific Terms

Review this information to learn about the different terms used in science. 

Scientific Data

In science, data is the essential, factual information collected through observation or experimentation. Think of it as the raw evidence that allows scientists to test an idea, track a trend, and ultimately understand the natural world. Data is the backbone of the entire scientific method; without it, a hypothesis is just a guess.

Two Types of Data

Scientific data is generally categorized into two major types:

  1. Quantitative Data (Numbers): This type of data deals with quantities—things that can be measured or counted. It is always expressed in numbers and usually includes specific units. Quantitative data is powerful because it allows for precise mathematical and statistical analysis. Here are some examples:
    • The temperature of an oven is 350°F
    • The mass of a rock 250 grams
    • The number of bacteria colonies in a petri dish is 80 colonies.
  1. Qualitative Data (Descriptions): This type of data deals with qualities or characteristics that are observed, not measured with an instrument. It is descriptive and often expressed in words.  This data provides important context and detail that numbers alone cannot capture; however, it this type of data can sometimes be vague. Here are some examples:
    • The color of a chemical change turned bright pink
    • The texture of the fossil is smooth and shiny
    • A patient's description of their pain is sharp, stabbing feeling.

The Role of Data in Science

The process of collecting data must be systematic and unbiased to ensure the results are reliable. Scientists organize this raw data into tables, charts, and graphs to look for patterns, trends, and relationships.

If the collected data consistently supports a hypothesis, it strengthens that idea. If the data repeatedly contradicts the hypothesis, it must be revised or rejected. Because data is objective evidence, it allows other scientists to replicate the experiment and verify the findings. This commitment to testable evidence and peer review is what distinguishes true science from mere opinion.

Hypothesis

A scientific hypothesis is the cornerstone of the entire scientific process. It is not just a random guess, but rather a proposed explanation for an observable phenomenon. Think of it as a tentative answer to a specific scientific question.

A Testable Statement

To be useful in science, a hypothesis must meet two critical criteria: it must be testable and falsifiable.

  • Testable: This means you must be able to design an experiment or make observations that can either support or reject the statement.
  • Falsifiable: This is the most crucial part. A true scientific hypothesis must be capable of being proven wrong. For example, the statement "All swans are white" is a testable and falsifiable hypothesis because finding just one black swan proves it false. A statement like "Invisible unicorns cause gravity" is not scientific because no experiment could ever prove it false.

Most hypotheses are structured as "If, Then" statements to clearly outline the expected relationship between variables. For example: "If plant A is given twice the amount of water as plant B, then plant A will grow taller." This format clearly links the independent variable (water amount) to the dependent variable (plant height).

Hypothesis vs. Everyday Language

In everyday conversation, people often use the word "hypothesis" interchangeably with "guess," "hunch," or "opinion." If you say, "I have a hypothesis that the new restaurant is going to fail," you are really just offering an opinion or a guess based on intuition. This guess doesn't require evidence, a formal test, or the possibility of being proven wrong in a controlled way.

In contrast, the scientific definition demands rigor. A scientific hypothesis is always grounded in existing knowledge and previous observations. It's an informed explanation that a scientist is prepared to subject to rigorous testing. If the test results do not support the hypothesis, the scientist rejects it and develops a new, refined one. This constant cycle of proposal and testing is what allows science to advance.

Scientific Model

Representation and Prediction

In science, a model is a simplified representation of a system, phenomenon, or process that is too complex, too large, or too small to observe directly. Models are critical tools used by scientists to help visualize, explain, and predict the behavior of the natural world.

A scientific model is not meant to be an exact copy of reality; rather, it is a purposeful simplification. Think of the Bohr model of the atom which models an atom like a solar system where the nucleus is like the star and the planets are like electrons. It turns out that this model works well for hydrogen-like atoms, but fails for atoms with more than one electron.

Scientific models come in many forms:

  • Physical Models: Scale replicas, like a 3D model of DNA or the human heart.
  • Conceptual Models: Analogies or diagrams, like a food web diagram in ecology.
  • Mathematical/Computer Models: Equations or simulations used to predict complex events, such as climate change, weather forecasts, or the spread of a disease.

Scientific Model vs. Everyday Model

The term "model" is used loosely in everyday conversation. When someone refers to a "model car," they mean a miniature copy of the real thing, intended primarily for appearance. When someone refers to a "fashion model," they mean a human demonstrating clothing. In both everyday cases, the model is judged by how closely it resembles or physically represents the real object.

In science, the model is judged not on its appearance or realism, but on its functionality—its ability to explain observations and make accurate predictions. If a scientist uses a mathematical model to predict the path of a hurricane, and the prediction is wrong, the model must be revised or rejected, regardless of how logical its initial equations seemed. This cycle of building, testing, and revising models is fundamental to how scientific knowledge advances.

Scientific Theory

The Highest Level of Certainty

A scientific theory represents the pinnacle of scientific understanding. It is a comprehensive, well-substantiated explanation of some major aspect of the natural world that has been repeatedly tested and confirmed through observation and experimentation.

Crucially, a scientific theory is not just a guess or a simple idea. It is a powerful framework that integrates many different facts, confirmed hypotheses, and laws to explain a broad set of related phenomena.

For example, the Theory of Plate Tectonics explains everything from earthquakes and volcanoes to the drift of continents by unifying observations about the Earth’s crust and mantle. Similarly, the Theory of Evolution explains the unity and diversity of life using evidence from genetics, fossils, and anatomy. A theory is not easily discarded; it remains valid only as long as it continues to explain new data and withstand rigorous testing. In fact, in science, a theory is the closest we get to a deep, reliable explanation of truth.

Here are some examples of notable scientific theories and what they explain:

  • Theory of Relativity - How gravity works, describing it not as a force but as a curvature in the fabric of spacetime caused by mass and energy. This theory is essential for modern astronomy and technologies like GPS.
  • Germ Theory of Disease - The fact that specific microorganisms (like bacteria, viruses, and fungi) are the cause of infectious diseases. This theory transformed medicine, leading to sanitation, vaccination, and antibiotics.
  • Big Bang Theory - The origin and subsequent expansion of the universe from an extremely hot, dense state. It is supported by evidence like cosmic background radiation and the observed redshift of distant galaxies.
  • Cell Theory - One of the foundational theories of biology, which states that all living things are composed of cells, the cell is the basic unit of life, and all cells arise from pre-existing cells.
  • Theory of Plate Tectonics - The movement of the Earth's rigid outer layer (lithosphere), which is broken into large plates. This movement explains virtually all large-scale geological phenomena, including earthquakes, volcanoes, and the formation of mountains and ocean trenches.
  • Theory of Evolution by Natural Selection - How populations of organisms change over time (evolution) and how new species arise. It is the unifying theory of biology, explaining life's diversity and common ancestry.
  • Kinetic Molecular Theory - The behavior of gases (and, to an extent, liquids and solids) based on the idea that matter is composed of tiny particles (atoms or molecules) that are constantly in motion. KMT explains why gases expand, how pressure increases with temperature, and how diffusion occurs.
  • Atomic Theory - The internal structure of the atom. Building upon the work of Dalton, Rutherford, and Bohr, the modern theory describes electrons not as orbiting planets, but as existing in probabilistic regions of space called orbitals.
  • Theory of Atmospheric Circulation - This theory explains the large-scale movement of air around the planet, driven by uneven solar heating between the equator and the poles. It defines major global wind patterns, such as the trade winds, westerlies, and polar easterlies, and the resulting pressure belts.

Theory vs. Everyday Language

The confusion often arises because the word "theory" is used so loosely outside of the lab. In everyday conversation, people often say, "I have a theory about why the team lost," when they really mean, "I have a hunch or a guess." In this casual context, an everyday "theory" is unproven, based on limited information, or simply an opinion.

The Scientific Method

The scientific method is the systematic process scientists use to explore observations and answer questions about the natural world. It is not just a list of steps, but a cycle of thinking and testing that allows us to distinguish fact from opinion.

It usually begins with a question based on an observation. To answer this, a scientist formulates a hypothesis. A hypothesis is often called an "educated guess," but more accurately, it is a testable prediction. It must be specific enough that it could be proven wrong.

To test the hypothesis, scientists design an experiment. This is a controlled procedure where variables are manipulated to see what happens. During the experiment, they collect data in the forms of measurements, numbers, and observations. This data provides the evidence needed to judge the hypothesis.

Once the data is analyzed, scientists often use a model to explain their findings. A model is a representation (physical, mathematical, or conceptual) of a system that helps us understand or simulate complex real-world phenomena, like the climate or the structure of an atom.

If a hypothesis is tested repeatedly over time and is consistently supported by a massive amount of data and evidence, it may become part of a scientific theory. In everyday language, "theory" often means a hunch, but in science, a theory is a comprehensive explanation of an aspect of nature that has withstood rigorous scrutiny, such as the Theory of Plate Tectonics or the Theory of Evolution.

Pseudoscience

Characteristics of Pseudoscience

Pseudoscience (pronounced soo-doh-science) refers to any set of beliefs, practices, or claims that pretends to be scientific but fundamentally lacks evidence, testability, and adherence to the scientific method. It is often dressed up with scientific-sounding jargon, but it is not science.

Unlike true science, which is open to revision, constantly seeks evidence, and corrects itself when new facts arise, pseudoscience is often characterized by several key traits:

  1. Fixed Beliefs: Pseudoscience ignores or actively dismisses any evidence that contradicts its initial claims. It does not evolve, as scientific theories do.

  2. Unfalsifiability: The claims are often worded in such a vague or generalized way that they cannot be proven wrong through experimentation. If a claim cannot be tested, it cannot be considered science.

  3. Reliance on Anecdotes: It frequently uses personal testimonials ("It worked for me!") instead of data collected from controlled, repeatable experiments.

  4. Lack of Peer Review: Pseudoscience typically avoids publishing its methods and results in respected scientific journals where they would be scrutinized by other experts.

Examples of Pseudoscience

A classic example is Astrology, the belief that the alignment of stars and planets influences human personality and future events. While astronomical data is real, the claim that it affects human life has repeatedly failed scientific testing. Another example is Ancient Astronaut Theory, which proposes that highly advanced alien beings visited Earth in the distant past and were responsible for major human technological advances. Although intriguing, this belief relies on speculation and reinterpretation of myths rather than on testable, physical evidence.

When considering if a belief is based on pseudoscience be on the look out for these red flags

  • Anecdotes: Relying on simple sensory input over instruments.
  • Fixed Beliefs: No amount of evidence changes the core belief.
  • Conspiracy: Explaining away failure as sabotage.

Proportionality

Proportionality is a fundamental mathematical concept that describes the relationship between two changing quantities, often represented as variables. It provides a framework for predicting how one quantity will react when another changes, serving as a cornerstone for all fields of science and general problem-solving.

Direct Proportionality

Two variables, let's call them x and y , are directly proportional if they change in the same direction at a constant rate. Simply put, if x doubles, y doubles; if x is halved, y is halved.

The mathematical relationship is expressed as:

y = k   x

Here, k is a non-zero constant known as the constant of proportionality. In this relationship, the ratio y x is always equal to k .

Visual and Real-World Example:

  • Graph: The graph of a direct proportion is a straight line that passes through the origin ( 0 ,  0 ) .

  • Example: Consider an hourly wage. If you earn $20 per hour, your total pay, y , is directly proportional to the hours worked, x . Working 5 hours yields $100; working 10 hours yields $200. The constant k is the wage rate $20 per hour.

Inverse Proportionality

Inverse proportionality describes an opposing relationship. As one variable increases, the other decreases in exact proportion, such that their product remains constant. If variable x is multiplied by 3, variable y is divided by 3.

The equation for this relationship is:

y = k x

Real-World Example:

  • Graph: The graph of an inverse proportion is a curve called a hyperbola, which never touches the x-axis or y-axis.

  • Example: Consider the time it takes to travel a fixed distance. If you are driving 100 miles, speed and time are inversely proportional. If you drive at 50 mph, it takes 2 hours. If you double your speed to 100 mph, the time is halved to 1 hour. The product of speed and time always equals the distance k = 100  miles .

Summary

The distinction lies in the interaction of the variables. In direct proportionality, the variables move in unison effectively maintaining a constant ratio. In inverse proportionality, they engage in a trade-off to maintain a constant product. recognizing these patterns allows us to model complex systems, from the expansion of recipes (direct) to the relationship between pressure and volume in a gas (inverse).

Statistically Significant

In the world of science and data, "statistically significant" is a phrase you hear often. It sounds like it means "majorly important," but in mathematics, it has a very specific technical meaning. It is a way of determining whether a result is likely due to a real cause or just random luck.

Probability

Imagine you are testing a new fertilizer to see if it makes plants grow taller. You treat one group of plants with the fertilizer and leave another group without it. After a month, the treated plants are an inch taller on average. Is the fertilizer working, or did you just happen to pick naturally taller seeds for that group?

Statistical significance helps answer this. It uses probability to calculate the odds that your results occurred by pure chance.

P-Value

The standard tool for measuring this is the p-value. The computation of the p-value is beyond the scope of this course, but you should know that value is used to set a threshold for classifying a result as "statistically significant" or not.

  • Low p-value (usually < 0.05): This means there is less than a 5% probability that your results are random noise. Scientists call this "statistically significant." It suggests there is real evidence that the fertilizer works.

  • High p-value (usually > 0.05): This means the results could easily happen by chance. We cannot confidently say the fertilizer had an effect.

Significance vs. Importance

It is crucial to understand that "significant" does not always mean "big" or "useful." If you study 1,000,000 people, you might find that a new medicine cures a headache 10 seconds faster than an old one. Because the study is so huge, this result might be statistically significant (it’s not random). However, curing a headache 10 seconds faster is not practically important to a patient.

Understanding precise scientific language, such as the distinction between "statistically significant" and "practically important," is a critical skill for technicians. It transforms them from simple operators into informed decision-makers who can bridge the gap between data and application.

Firstly, it prevents the misinterpretation of operational data. A technician reading a quality control report might see that a process deviation is "statistically significant." Without the proper vocabulary, they might panic, assuming a major failure. However, understanding the term allows them to realize this simply means the deviation isn't random—it might still be well within safe tolerances.

Secondly, it aids in efficient resource management; a result can be significant without being useful. A technician who grasps this concept will not waste time or budget chasing "statistically significant" optimizations that result in negligible real-world performance gains, focusing instead on changes that offer practical value.
